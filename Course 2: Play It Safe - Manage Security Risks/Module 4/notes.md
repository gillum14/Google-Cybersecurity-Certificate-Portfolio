## Reading: More About Playbooks

### Playbook Overview

A playbook is a manual providing a predefined, up-to-date list of steps to perform when responding to an incident. Playbooks are accompanied by a strategy that outlines expectations for assigned team members (and sometimes who's responsible), paired with a plan dictating how the task should be completed. Playbooks are living documents, updated collaboratively as team members bring different levels of expertise. Updates typically happen when a failure or oversight is identified, industry standards/regulations change, or the threat landscape evolves.

### Types of Playbooks

Playbooks often cover specific incidents and vulnerabilities — ransomware, vishing, BEC, and others. Incident and vulnerability response playbooks are the most common type, though organizations develop others too. Playbook content, tools, and procedures vary by organization and even by country, since notification requirements from local laws/regulations affect what's included.

### Incident and Vulnerability Response Playbooks

Developed based on the goals in an organization's business continuity plan (an established path for a business to recover and keep operating despite a disruption like a breach). Both types contain predefined, up-to-date response steps — following them ensures legal/organizational compliance, minimizes errors, and keeps actions within a specific timeframe. Because risk level depends on potential damage to assets, a sense of urgency is essential, and following playbook steps carefully protects forensic data from being mishandled and rendered unusable.

Common steps included in incident/vulnerability response playbooks:

- Preparation
- Detection
- Analysis
- Containment
- Eradication
- Recovery from an incident

Additional steps include post-incident activities and coordinating efforts throughout the investigation and response stages.

**Key takeaway:** Cybersecurity teams should continually refine playbooks based on what's learned from each documented incident, considering what improvements would help handle future incidents more effectively. Playbooks create structure and ensure compliance with the law.

## Reading: Playbooks, SIEM Tools, and SOAR Tools

### Playbooks and SIEM Tools

Playbooks help security teams respond to incidents consistently, regardless of who's working the case — they can include flow charts and tables clarifying what actions to take and in what order. Playbooks are also used for recovery procedures, such as in a ransomware attack, with different incident types having their own dedicated playbooks. Playbooks are generally used alongside SIEM tools: if a SIEM flags unusual user behavior, the relevant playbook tells the analyst how to address it.

### Playbooks and SOAR Tools

Playbooks are also used with SOAR tools, which automate repetitive tasks generated by tools like a SIEM or managed detection and response (MDR) system. For example, if a user fails too many login attempts, a SOAR tool might automatically block the account to stop a possible intrusion — analysts then consult the playbook to resolve the issue from there.

**Key takeaway:** Playbooks (sometimes called runbooks) provide detailed, role-specific actions for a team to take during an incident. Knowing exactly who does what and when reduces the impact of an incident and the risk of damage to an organization's critical assets.

## Personal Reflection

This module clarified something I'd been fuzzy on — the relationship between SIEM, SOAR, and playbooks isn't one tool doing everything; it's a division of labor. The SIEM detects and flags, SOAR automates the immediate/repetitive response, and the playbook is the human-readable guide that tells an analyst exactly what to do next. Framing playbooks as "living documents" that get revised after every incident also reframed them for me as something the team owns and improves, not a static manual handed down once.
