## Reading: The Purpose and Impact of Stakeholders

A **stakeholder** is an individual or group with an interest in any decision or activity of an organization. Reporting findings to stakeholders is a core part of a security analyst's job.

### Levels of Stakeholders

- **Cybersecurity risk manager:** Leads efforts to identify, assess, and mitigate security risks.
- **CEO:** The highest-ranking person in the organization — unlikely to be a direct point of contact for an entry-level analyst.
- **CFO:** Focused on financial impact (e.g., the potential cost of an incident) — also unlikely to be a direct contact.
- **CISO:** The highest-level security stakeholder — also unlikely to be a direct contact.
- **Operations manager:** Oversees day-to-day security operations — a stakeholder entry-level analysts are more likely to communicate with regularly.

### Stakeholder Communications for Entry-Level Analysts

Analysts typically report to operations managers and risk managers, clearly communicating the current issue and its possible causes; those stakeholders then determine next steps and coordinate remediation — sometimes with the analyst's help implementing preventative measures.

### From One Stakeholder to the Next

Operations and risk managers report up to CISOs/CFOs to give a broader picture of the organization's security posture — so even without direct contact with senior stakeholders, an analyst's day-to-day work still reaches the top levels of the organization.

**Key takeaway:** Entry-level analysts won't communicate directly with every stakeholder level, but understanding the full hierarchy clarifies why clear, accurate reporting to immediate stakeholders (like an operations manager) matters — that information travels upward.

## Reading: Communicate Effectively with Stakeholders

### Get to the Point

Before communicating, ask: What do I want this person to know? Why does it matter to them? When do they need to act? How do I explain it in nontechnical terms?

### Follow the Protocols

Learn an organization's accepted communication channels and procedures before communicating with stakeholders (in-person, video, email, chat tools).

### Communicate with Impact

Tailor the message to the stakeholder's specific focus — operations managers care about day-to-day details (e.g., log anomalies being escalated), while senior stakeholders are more interested in underlying risk (e.g., potential financial impact) than granular logging details.

### Communication Methods

Choose the method based on the type of information: instant messaging or a phone call for something straightforward; email or an in-person meeting for something complex with multiple layers; a graph or shared spreadsheet when sharing data and numbers.

**Key takeaway:** Effective stakeholder communication means being concise, following established protocols, tailoring the message to the audience's specific interests, and matching the communication method to the complexity of the information.

## Reading: Create Visual Dashboards for Impactful Cybersecurity Communications

**Visual dashboards** display various types of data quickly in one place, helping busy stakeholders quickly grasp actionable information — especially valuable when communicating numbers/data-heavy findings, since stakeholders are often occupied with other responsibilities. Dashboards can be simple (a single chart) or complex (multiple charts/graphs/tables), depending on the story being told; tools like Google Sheets or Apache OpenOffice can be used to build them.

### When to Use Visual Communication

Sometimes a quick email or phone call is enough; other times — especially when the finding involves numbers, like phishing-click results across five departments over several months — a chart or graph communicates the story far more clearly and quickly than a written description alone.

**Key takeaway:** Recognizing when a finding is better shown than described — particularly for numeric/data-heavy results — is a key communication skill, since visuals help stakeholders act quickly on what they see.

## Reading: Strategies for Engaging with the Cybersecurity Community

Cybersecurity evolves rapidly, making ongoing community engagement important even after finishing formal coursework.

### Security Organizations and Conferences

- **Find the right organization:** Narrow down by specific interest (incident response, forensic security, data logging, etc.) before searching.
- **Begin the search:** Web searches like "incident response cybersecurity conferences in my area" surface relevant local options.
- **Use social media:** LinkedIn is useful for finding security groups/organizations via targeted searches.
- **Be aware of social engineering:** Social media is also a common vector for attacks — avoid clicking unexpected links/attachments from unfamiliar users, even while networking.

### Mailing Lists for Security

CISA offers mailing lists covering security threat information/best practices, and weekly summaries of new vulnerabilities.

**Key takeaway:** Conferences, organizations, social media, and mailing lists are all low-effort, ongoing ways to stay current in a field that changes quickly — matched to a person's specific area of interest within security.

## Reading: Connect with Other Cybersecurity Professionals

### Following CISOs on LinkedIn

CISOs are typically well informed across every aspect of security — following them surfaces useful tips, trends, and news that support sharpening a security mindset over time.

### Finding Other Security Professionals on LinkedIn

When reaching out to connect, use a conversational tone, give a clear reason for connecting, and avoid spelling/grammar errors — this signals genuine intent rather than a scam attempt. Example message: *"Hi, Tim. I recently completed the Google Cybersecurity Certificate program, and I'd like to connect with other security professionals. It seems like you have a lot of experience in the security industry that I can learn from. Let's keep in touch!"*

**Key takeaway:** Thoughtful, specific outreach messages (not generic or suspicious-looking ones) are the difference between building genuine professional connections and being ignored — or mistaken for a scammer.

## Reading: Tips for Finding and Applying for a Job

- **ZipRecruiter:** Matches job seekers to roles based on location/work preferences; employers can reach out directly based on an uploaded resume/profile.
- **Indeed:** Search by job title, keyword, or company plus preferred location; recruiters can reach out based on an uploaded resume.
- **Monster:** Frequently used in the U.S. and internationally; same core search-and-upload-resume approach.
- **LinkedIn:** Use the "jobs" tab to search by location and title; also useful for researching a company's culture, values, and community initiatives to assess fit.

**Key takeaway:** Combining job search platforms with professional networking (LinkedIn) gives both direct job leads and insight into whether a company is actually the right fit.

## Reading: The Interview Process

### Getting Contacted by a Recruiter

Respond promptly to show responsiveness and interest.

### Preliminary Interview or Phone Screening

Typically shorter, covering company/role basics and initial questions about background/experience. Prepare a few questions in advance (e.g., about work culture or a typical day) to show genuine interest and assess fit. Send a thank-you email within 24 hours.

### Additional Interviews

Often with the hiring manager and possibly future teammates, sometimes including a technical interview; may involve multiple rounds. Send a thank-you note after each round.

### Final Offer

May come by phone or email; it's reasonable to ask for a day or two to decide, and to negotiate salary/benefits.

### Building Perseverance

If not selected, it's fine to express appreciation for the opportunity, note interest in future roles, and ask for feedback on how to improve.

**Key takeaway:** The interview process typically follows a predictable arc — recruiter contact, preliminary screening, additional rounds, and an offer — with thank-you notes and thoughtful questions at each stage helping build a positive, professional impression throughout.

## Reading: Apply the STAR Method During Interviews

The **STAR method** — Situation, Task, Action, Result — helps structure answers that clearly demonstrate how a challenge was handled from start to finish:

- **Situation:** The project or challenge faced (e.g., managing a customer complaint, a system error, being left alone in the office).
- **Task:** The specific responsibility or role played in addressing it.
- **Action:** The exact steps taken to resolve the situation — this is the part that shows an interviewer how you think and make decisions under real conditions.
- **Result:** How the situation was resolved as a direct result of those actions — ideally a positive outcome, though if asked about a less successful outcome, focus on what was learned and how it improved future performance.

**Key takeaway:** The STAR method turns a vague "tell me about a challenge you faced" prompt into a structured, credible story — and since cybersecurity is such a team-driven, problem-heavy field, being able to walk through a challenge clearly is itself a demonstration of the communication skills the job requires.
