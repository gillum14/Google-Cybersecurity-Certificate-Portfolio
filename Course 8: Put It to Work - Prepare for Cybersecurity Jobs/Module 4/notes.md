# Course 8, Module 4 – Engage with the Cybersecurity Community

## Overview

This module covered how to stay current in a fast-evolving industry through security news resources, and how to actively engage with the cybersecurity community — through conferences/organizations, professional social media networking, and mailing lists.

---

## Learning Objectives

After completing this module, I can:

- Explain why staying current with industry trends and news matters for security professionals
- Identify valuable ongoing security news resources
- Describe strategies for finding cybersecurity organizations, conferences, and mailing lists
- Apply best practices for professional networking on social media, including with CISOs

---

## Reading: The Evolving Security Landscape

Cybersecurity is constantly changing — for example, the OWASP Top 10 is updated every three to four years to reflect new risks. Staying current with industry trends and news is essential for remaining competitive and effective as a security professional.

### Valuable Security Resources

- **CSO Online:** News, analysis, and research on security and risk management, aimed at Chief Security Officers.
- **Krebs on Security:** An in-depth blog by Brian Krebs covering security news and investigations into cyberattacks.
- **Dark Reading:** A popular resource for security professionals covering analytics, application security, mobile/cloud security, and IoT.

**Key takeaway:** Because the field changes constantly, keeping a rotation of a few trusted, ongoing security news sources is a low-effort way to stay current after finishing formal coursework.

## Reading: Strategies for Engaging with the Cybersecurity Community

### Security Organizations and Conferences

- **Find the right organization:** Clarify your specific interest first — incident response vs. prevention, forensic security, data logging, or a longer-term goal like becoming a CISO — before searching.
- **Begin the search:** Use targeted web searches like "incident response cybersecurity conferences in my area" or "forensic security organizations in my area."
- **Use social media:** LinkedIn search queries like "incident response cybersecurity groups" or "organizations for cybersecurity analysts" can surface relevant groups.
- **Be aware of social engineering:** Social media is also a common attack vector — never click unexpected links/attachments from unfamiliar users, even while networking for professional purposes.

### Mailing Lists for Security

CISA offers two mailing lists: one covering security threat information, best practices, and analysis from domestic/international partners, and one providing weekly summaries of new vulnerabilities.

**Key takeaway:** Conferences, organizations, LinkedIn groups, and mailing lists are all ways to stay current — the right ones depend on identifying your specific security interest first, and staying alert to social engineering even while networking.

## Reading: Using Social Media for Professional Networking

- **Follow industry leaders:** Connect with CISOs on LinkedIn to gain insights from their posts, interviews, and shared articles.
- **Connect with analysts:** Search LinkedIn for cybersecurity analysts (e.g., using "#cybersecurity") and send connection requests with a brief, personalized message to learn about their experience.

### Alternative Networking Strategies

For those less active on social media, researching and joining cybersecurity industry associations aligned with professional goals is another path to staying engaged.

**Key takeaway:** Professional networking doesn't require heavy social media use — following industry leaders and connecting with analysts is one path, while joining an industry association is an equally valid alternative.

---

## Reading: Connect with Other Cybersecurity Professionals

### LinkedIn with CISOs

CISOs stay current on every aspect of security, so following them on LinkedIn surfaces useful tips and relevant news that can help sharpen a security mindset over time.

### Finding Other Security Professionals on LinkedIn

When reaching out, use a conversational tone, give a clear reason for connecting, and avoid spelling/grammar errors — this helps the recipient trust your intentions rather than mistake you for a scammer. Example message: *"Hi, Tim. I recently completed the Google Cybersecurity Certificate program, and I'd like to connect with other security professionals. It seems like you have a lot of experience in the security industry that I can learn from. Let's keep in touch!"*

**Key takeaway:** CISOs are especially valuable to follow since they're responsible for all aspects of an organization's security and often share broadly useful tips/trends — and a thoughtful, specific outreach message is what separates a genuine connection request from something that looks suspicious.

## Personal Reflection

The reminder to stay alert to social engineering *while networking* stuck with me — it's easy to let guard down in a context that feels professional and low-risk (LinkedIn, industry groups), but that's exactly the kind of setting attackers exploit.
