## Overview

This module built on the eight CISSP security domains by examining specific methods of attack and the individuals and groups behind them. It covered common attack types, the threat actors who carry them out, and their varying motivations and skill levels.

## Reading: Determine the Type of Attack

### Password Attack

An attempt to access password-secured devices, systems, networks, or data. Common forms include brute force and rainbow table attacks. Falls under the **communication and network security** domain.

### Social Engineering Attack

A manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain private information, access, or valuables. Common forms include phishing, smishing, vishing, spear phishing, whaling, social media phishing, Business Email Compromise (BEC), watering hole attacks, USB baiting, and physical social engineering. Falls under the **security and risk management** domain.

### Physical Attack

A security incident affecting both digital and physical environments, such as malicious USB cables or flash drives, and card cloning/skimming. Falls under the **asset security** domain.

### Adversarial Artificial Intelligence

A technique that manipulates AI/ML technology to conduct attacks more efficiently. Falls under both the **communication and network security** and **identity and access management** domains.

### Supply-Chain Attack

Targets systems, applications, hardware, and/or software to locate a vulnerability where malware can be deployed. Because every product passes through multiple third parties, a breach can occur at any point in the chain. Can fall under several domains, including security and risk management, security architecture and engineering, and security operations.

### Cryptographic Attack

Affects secure communication between a sender and intended recipient. Common forms include birthday, collision, and downgrade attacks. Falls under the **communication and network security** domain.

**Key takeaways:** The eight CISSP domains help an organization prepare for and fortify against data breaches. Breaches range from simple to complex and can fall under one or more domains. The attack types above are only a sample of many methods threat actors use.

## Reading: Understand Attackers

### Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

Have significant expertise accessing an organization's network without authorization. Tend to research targets (large corporations or government entities) in advance and can remain undetected for extended periods. Intentions/motivations include damaging critical infrastructure (e.g., the power grid) and gaining access to intellectual property such as trade secrets or patents.

### Insider Threats

Abuse authorized access to obtain data that may harm an organization. Motivations include sabotage, corruption, espionage, and unauthorized data access or leaks.

### Hacktivists

Driven by a political agenda. Abuse digital technology to accomplish goals such as demonstrations, propaganda, social change campaigns, or fame.

### Hacker Types

A hacker is any person who uses computers to gain access to computer systems, networks, or data — the term alone doesn't imply intent. Three main categories:

- **Authorized (ethical) hackers:** Follow a code of ethics and the law to conduct organizational risk evaluations, motivated to safeguard people and organizations from malicious threat actors.
- **Semi-authorized hackers:** Considered researchers — they search for vulnerabilities but don't take advantage of what they find.
- **Unauthorized (unethical) hackers:** Malicious threat actors who don't follow or respect the law, aiming to collect and sell confidential data for financial gain.

Note: some hackers span more than one category. New/unskilled threat actors may be motivated to learn and enhance hacking skills, seek revenge, or exploit weaknesses using existing malware and scripts. Others aren't driven by a particular agenda and simply complete contracted jobs — legal or illegal. Some consider themselves vigilantes, aiming to protect others from unethical hackers.

**Key takeaway:** Threat actors are defined by malicious intent; hackers are defined by technical skill and motivation. Understanding both helps in preparing to protect an organization and the people it serves.

## Glossary Terms from Module 2

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Adversarial artificial intelligence (AI) | A technique that manipulates AI/ML technology to conduct attacks more efficiently |
| Business Email Compromise (BEC) | A phishing attack where a threat actor impersonates a known source to obtain financial advantage |
| CISSP | Certified Information Systems Security Professional — a globally recognized information security certification |
| Computer virus | Malicious code written to interfere with computer operations and damage data/software |
| Cryptographic attack | An attack that affects secure communication between a sender and intended recipient |
| Hacker | Any person who uses computers to gain access to computer systems, networks, or data |
| Malware | Software designed to harm devices or networks |
| Password attack | An attempt to access password-secured devices, systems, networks, or data |
| Phishing | The use of digital communications to trick people into revealing sensitive data or deploying malicious software |
| Physical attack | A security incident affecting both digital and physical environments |
| Physical social engineering | An attack where a threat actor impersonates an employee, customer, or vendor to gain unauthorized physical access |
| Social engineering | A manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain private information, access, or valuables |
| Social media phishing | An attack where a threat actor gathers detailed target information from social media before attacking |
| Spear phishing | A malicious email attack targeting a specific user or group, appearing to come from a trusted source |
| Supply-chain attack | An attack targeting systems, applications, hardware, and/or software to locate an exploitable vulnerability |
| USB baiting | An attack where a threat actor leaves a malware-infected USB stick for an employee to find and install |
| Vishing | The exploitation of electronic voice communication to obtain sensitive information or impersonate a known source |
| Watering hole attack | An attack where a threat actor compromises a website frequently visited by a specific group of users |
